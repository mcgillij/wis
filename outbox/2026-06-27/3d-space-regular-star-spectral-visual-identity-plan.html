Visual Briefs

O and B Stars

Use compact, intensely blue-white surface color, strong bloom, and visible stellar wind. Surface texture should be subtle; the readability comes from harsh blue lighting, large corona, and maybe wind streaks. O stars should be rare and dangerous; B stars can be common enough as bright navigational anchors. Avoid black-hole-style lensing.

A Stars

Clean white stars with pale blue rim and crisp corona. Low spot density and a polished look distinguish them from granular G/K stars. Good hosts for debris disks or exocomet phenomena.

F Stars

Warm white/gold, slightly hotter and brighter than G. Fine-grained surface noise, moderate corona, lower spot contrast. Use as a subtle but meaningful variation for habitable systems.

G Stars

Baseline solar visual: yellow-white core, visible granulation, moderate starspots, looping prominences, balanced corona. This should be the reference calibration for bloom and exposure.

K Stars

Orange-gold, stable, cozy but dimmer. Slightly larger apparent limb softness and slower surface animation. Excellent for readable warm systems without extreme hazards.

M Dwarfs

Small red ember with high spot coverage. Some should be quiet; flare-star variants should pulse with red/white flares and CME particles. Keep local illumination dim and red-tinted to make the system feel different.

Giants and AGB Stars

Scale is the hook: huge soft disk, slow convection cells, dusty circumstellar shell, mass-loss particles, and possible detached arcs. AGB variants can layer into the engulfment/rare-phenomena reports.

Supergiants / Hypergiants

Use bespoke scale treatment: large backdrop footprint, unstable lumpy surface, extended halo, wind-blown shell, and heavy exposure management. They should feel different from merely scaling up a normal star sphere.

  • O/B: high temperature, high luminosity, low spot density, strong corona/wind.
  • F/G/K: moderate temperature, useful granulation, balanced spots and prominences.
  • M: low temperature, low luminosity, high spot density, optional high flare rate.
  • Giants: high radius scale, lower surface flow speed, dust shell and limb softness.

Regular vs Exotic Readability

  • Regular stars should be spherical, emissive, and atmosphere/corona-driven.
  • Exotics own the extreme silhouettes: event-horizon lensing, narrow beams, accretion-dominated disks, impossible dark cores.
  • White dwarfs sit near the boundary: regular remnant scientifically, but game-facing exotic because of small intense silhouette and debris disks.
  • Use scan labels and map icons for edge cases so art does not carry all classification burden.

Godot Techniques

  • MeshInstance3D sphere: emissive shader with layered noise and limb darkening.
  • Layered transparent shells: corona, prominences, halo, and dust.
  • Particles: flares, CME arcs, wind streaks, giant mass loss.
  • SubViewport/impostors: cache far star appearances for star maps and distant billboards.
  • Color ramps: map spectral class to physically inspired but game-readable palettes.
  • LOD: far impostor, mid simplified shells, near full particles/surface.
